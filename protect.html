Overview

DA offices running on spreadsheets and paper

When I joined the PROTECT project, Wisconsin District Attorney offices were tracking active criminal cases across a combination of spreadsheets, shared drives, and paper files. There was no single source of truth, no audit trail, and no way to understand case workload at a glance.

The State of Wisconsin's Department of Administration needed a purpose-built case management system. I was brought in as the sole UI/UX designer and embedded with the team for 18 months — long enough to understand the domain deeply, build trust with stakeholders, and deliver five distinct design efforts under the PROTECT umbrella.

My primary challenge was that desktop and web users had fundamentally different interaction patterns. DAs reviewing case files needed dense, information-rich desktop interfaces. Supervisors and clerks accessing the web portal needed streamlined task flows. One IA would not serve both. Solving that split became the structural foundation for every design decision that followed.

Problem & Solution

What was broken, and how we fixed it

⚠️  Before PROTECT
  • Cases tracked in Excel — no audit trail, version conflicts
  • No visibility into DA workload across the office
  • eFiling done via email attachments — error-prone, manual
  • Desktop and web users forced into the same interface
  • No brand or visual identity — staff felt it wasn't a "real" system
  • XCQ queries ran in a separate legacy tool with no UI
✦  After PROTECT
  • Single platform with full case lifecycle tracking and audit log
  • Dashboard-driven workload visibility for supervisors
  • Dedicated eFiling portal with validation and status tracking
  • Separate IA for desktop (dense/keyboard) and web (task-focused)
  • Full brand identity: logo, colour system, typography, icon set
  • XCQ querying rebuilt as a first-class UI module

Discovery & Research

Map the lifecycle before touching the UI

Before sketching a single screen, I spent the first six weeks running discovery workshops with District Attorneys, investigators, administrative clerks, and IT stakeholders. The goal was to understand the full case lifecycle — not just the task flows, but the mental model each user type carried into their work.

The most important finding came from observing DAs vs. clerks side by side: DAs think in cases (deep, contextual, non-linear) while clerks think in tasks (linear, batch-oriented, time-sensitive). The same data set, accessed with completely different intent. This observation became the structural principle the entire IA was built on.

"When I'm reviewing a case I need everything — charges, history, filings, related parties — all visible without switching screens. I can't afford to hunt for information when I'm in court prep."
— District Attorney, Milwaukee County (research session, March 2024)
"I process 40–60 filings a day. I just need to know what's new, what needs action, and what's done. I don't need to see the whole case file every time."
— Administrative Clerk, Dane County (contextual inquiry, April 2024)
"Right now I run XCQ queries in a terminal. Nobody else in the office knows how. That knowledge shouldn't live in one person's head."
— Senior Investigator (stakeholder workshop, March 2024)

Key Design Decisions

The calls that shaped the platform

Split IA for desktop vs. web. The most important structural decision. Desktop users (DAs, investigators) needed dense, contextual, keyboard-first interfaces. Web users (clerks, supervisors) needed streamlined task flows optimised for high-volume daily use. One IA would have served neither group well. The split doubled the design effort but produced two genuinely usable experiences.

Auto-populated fields in eFiling. Early prototypes showed users re-entering case information that already existed in the system — leading to errors. Locking case fields and auto-populating from the case record eliminated an entire category of filing mistakes. This required close coordination with the dev team to expose the right data in the right form context.

Inline XCQ results, not a separate page. The original brief called for XCQ results to open in a new page. After testing with investigators, we found that users frequently needed to cross-reference results against other cases — requiring constant back-navigation. Moving results inline kept the query context visible and reduced the cognitive load of the task significantly.

Learnings

What I'd do differently

Start brand identity earlier. I designed the PROTECT brand identity in parallel with early app screens, which caused some rework when brand decisions evolved. In future embedded engagements, I'd lock brand fundamentals — colour, typography, tone — before any interface design begins.

Get DA office hours sooner. The most valuable research came from sitting with DAs during actual court prep sessions. That access took months to arrange through bureaucratic channels. Next time I'd negotiate observational access as a project kickoff requirement rather than an optional research activity.

Document design rationale as you go. Because the engagement was so long, later decisions sometimes lost the thread back to original research. Maintaining a running decision log — "why we chose X over Y" — would have made stakeholder reviews faster and reduced re-litigation of settled decisions.
