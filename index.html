How I Work

From ambiguity
to shipped product

Senior UX isn't about making things pretty. It's about making the right call under constraint, bringing stakeholders with you, and delivering work that earns trust — from users, from engineering, from business. Here's how I approach it.

01
Discover &
Frame

Before designing, I need to understand the actual problem — not the stated one. I run stakeholder interviews, contextual inquiry, heuristic audits, and competitive analysis to build a shared understanding of what's broken and why. Most redesigns fail because they solve the symptom. I find the system.

Stakeholder interviews Contextual inquiry Heuristic audit Journey mapping
02
Research &
Synthesise

I turn raw research into actionable design direction. User interviews, usability testing, and synthesis sessions produce not just insights but hypotheses — testable assumptions about what a better experience looks like. Every design decision maps back to evidence, not instinct.

User interviews Usability testing Affinity mapping FigJam synthesis
03
Design &
Validate

I design in layers — IA and flows first, lo-fi wireframes to validate structure, hi-fi to validate visual hierarchy and interaction patterns. I prototype in Figma or Axure RP, test with real users, and iterate fast. I've learned that the most important design decision is often which option not to pursue.

Wireframing Figma prototypes Axure RP 10 A/B concept testing
04
Build &
Ship

My front-end background means I work directly with engineering — not just hand off and hope. I write detailed specs, annotate edge cases, review builds in staging, and push back when implementation diverges from intent. The design that ships is the one that matters. Everything before it is just a hypothesis.

Design systems Dev handoff WCAG 2.1 AA React review

Lead Designer · Sole UX — End-to-End
Missouri DESE School Finance

Finance staff at 500+ districts used a 2003-era system — 4+ hours per submission, no error guidance, no save. I led discovery to React handoff as the sole designer. Research with 12 finance directors revealed the core issue wasn't the UI — it was the submission mental model. Districts thought in fiscal years; the system was structured by data field type. Restructuring IA around that insight drove the 40–50% time reduction.

HP MPS Shipping Dashboard

HP's managed print team had no real-time visibility across 247 devices. The constraint: two completely different user types (sales reps vs. contract managers) needed the same data, differently prioritised. Rather than one bloated dashboard, I designed two linked platforms — PMPS for pipeline, CMPS for contracts — with shared data architecture. Taught myself Axure RP 10 mid-project to meet engineering's prototyping requirements.

Citibank Spotlight

Six Citi teams across four tools, no shared view of who owned what. Discovery revealed the real problem: teams organised work by project type, but accountability broke down at the module level. Restructuring IA around module ownership — not project category — resolved the "who owns this?" loop. Built on Citi's existing design system; zero new components, full brand compliance, WCAG AA in both themes.

05
FedEx Office Category UX

High drop-off on product category pages despite correct product discovery. Heuristic audit pointed to pricing confusion — we designed two competing PDP concepts around different hierarchy assumptions. Moderated testing with 10 users showed Concept B won, but not for the reason we designed it. Users ignored the pricing widget we'd prioritised; they made purchase decisions on the service comparison table. That finding reshaped the final design and taught me that the insight is rarely in the assumption.

About Me

Designer, creator,
constant learner

Hi, I'm Shivani — a designer and artist based in Texas with roots in India. I came into UX through front-end code, which means I've always designed with real constraints in mind. Eight years later, I work in the spaces most designers avoid: complex government systems, internal banking tools, enterprise logistics.

Right now I'm working with the State of Missouri (DESE) as the solo UI/UX designer, handling the full redesign of the School Finance web application. Before that, I was embedded with the State of Wisconsin on the PROTECT Case Management System for 1.5 years — covering logo design, desktop app, web apps, XCQ, and eFiling across five major design efforts, all solo.

Before that, at HP Managed Print Services, I taught myself Axure RP 10 mid-project just to meet the team's needs and delivered 30+ screens on schedule. That project taught me a lot about leading design, staying user-focused, and collaborating closely with engineering. Over the years, I've also worked at Atos, Synechron, and others — delivering for FedEx and Citibank.

I studied Information Technology at Yeshwantrao Chavan College of Engineering in India, where a human-centered design internship started an obsession that never stopped.

Outside of work: old Bollywood music (nothing like Lata or Kishore to wind down), experimenting in the kitchen on weekends, and apparently winning design competitions.

I didn't start in UX. I started writing code — and somewhere between building interfaces and watching people struggle to use them, I realised the problem wasn't the code. It was the design.

That pivot, about 8 years ago, is still the best decision I've made. I now work at the intersection I've always cared about: where complex systems meet real people who just need things to work.

Outside of work, I'm a mum in Frisco, TX — which gives you a very sharp sense of what "intuitive" actually means.
