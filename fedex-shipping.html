A note on scope & my role
FedEx Office is a large enterprise product. This case study reflects my specific contribution as a junior UI Developer transitioning into UX design within an Atos Syntel project team. I contributed to UX research, wireframing, prototyping, and front-end implementation — not end-to-end product ownership. All research data and screens shown are from project work I participated in.
Background

The Project

FedEx Office provides print, ship, and business services both online and in-store across the US. The project goal was clear: make online shipping and print ordering fast and easy for FedEx customers worldwide.

The website was evolving toward a marketplace model with retail products from third-party suppliers. As part of the Atos Syntel team, we focused on a specific but high-impact problem: the product category landing page experience for FedEx Office’s print products — things like yard signs, banners, and business cards.

This was my first professional project. I joined as a UI Developer and, over four years, grew into UX design contributions — moving from building interfaces to helping shape what those interfaces should be.

Central Design Question
How might we make online print ordering fast, clear, and confidence-building
for FedEx customers around the world?
Problem Statement

What Customers Were Struggling With

Voice of Customer surveys revealed that customers had significant difficulty understanding the print products FedEx Office offered. The problem was two-fold: they couldn’t find all print options available, and when they did find them, they couldn’t distinguish between the variations.

🔍
Product discoverability failure
Customers couldn’t find all print options supplied by FedEx Office through the existing navigation and product pages. Options were buried inside dropdowns with no visual differentiation.
🤔
Confusion between product types
Users struggled to understand the differences between similar products — for example, Corrugated H-Stake vs. Corrugated H-Frame vs. Aluminum H-Frame yard signs. Three different products, no clear explanation.
💲
Pricing too late in the journey
Customers demanded pricing options much earlier in the customer journey. They were reaching the product details page before seeing any cost information, which eroded trust and caused drop-off.
📚
Poor information hierarchy on PDP
The product detail page had poor grid alignment, inconsistent spacing, both vertical and horizontal scrolling, and no explanatory visual content to help users understand what the product actually was.

Key Insights

“Customers required a better comprehension of available products, their options, and their specifications. Many had questions about why certain options weren’t offered, or wanted to know more about the materials used in the products.”
Voice of Customer · Survey synthesis
“Customers demanded pricing options much earlier in the customer journey.” Arriving at a product page with no price visible — or having to configure first before seeing cost — created anxiety and drop-off.
Key finding from VoC · Journey analysis
The website had both vertical and horizontal scrolling simultaneously, a glitch in video play buttons, poor grid alignment, repeated navigation options, and no explanatory visual content to help users understand what FedEx Office actually offers.

Competitive Context

Brand vs. Customer Needs

We mapped the tension between what FedEx needed as a brand and what customers actually cared about — and also assessed the competitive landscape. This shaped our design priorities: the experience had to serve both sides.

🌟 Brand Needs
  • → Fast, secure, and accurate delivery
  • → Safety and product quality standards
  • → Efficient routing and tracking infrastructure
  • → Global network utilisation and team coordination
  • → Technology and innovation investment
👤 Customer Needs
  • → Safe and timely door delivery
  • → Cost effectiveness & pricing upfront
  • → Real-time package tracking
  • → User-friendly website & clear product info
  • → Multiple payment options & responsive support
▶ Strengths
  • → World’s largest logistics company — 220+ nations
  • → Business-focused targeting with long-term partnerships
  • → Single pickup/delivery network maximises efficiency
  • → Heavy investment in technology and innovation
▲ Key Challenges
  • → Narrow trust gap vs. competitors BlueDart & UPS
  • → Digital marketing and self-serve UX lagging behind
  • → Managing traffic complexity in tracking and invoicing
  • → Over-dependence on US market for revenue growth

Redesigned Category Landing Page — Shop by Type
The core design solution was a “Shop by Type” section on the category landing page. Instead of a dropdown of identical-looking text options, each product type got its own card with a photo, starting price, and bullet points explaining what makes it different. This directly addressed the two primary pain points: discoverability and product differentiation. Pricing is surfaced immediately on the card — no need to configure first.

Tabbed Product Pricing Section
Directly below the Shop by Type cards, the category landing page included a tabbed pricing section that let users compare all three product types’ size and pricing options side-by-side — without navigating away. Each tab showed a visual diagram of the sign type, a pricing table with all size/side combinations, and a contextual G7 quality badge. This addressed the VoC finding that customers wanted pricing much earlier in their journey.

Experimental Concept B — Tooltip Guide on PDP
We recognised that many users land directly on a product detail page via organic search, bypassing the category page entirely. For these users, we tested two experimental designs. Concept B was closer to the existing PDP but added a tooltip-style guide on the “Yard Sign Type” dropdown — a “?” icon that expands to show all three options with prices, descriptions, and visual labels. This gives contextual help without forcing the user to navigate away.
Contextual help without leaving the page All 3 types compared with prices inline Familiar PDP layout preserved 6/10 users preferred this over visual menu
Usability Testing & Validation

Testing What Actually Worked

We ran two rounds of usability testing. The first compared the redesigned category landing page against the existing PDP as the primary navigation destination. The second tested two experimental PDP concepts against each other for users who landed directly on a product page.

Round 1: “When choosing a product from the nav — e.g. Yard Signs — which path do you prefer and why?”
8/10

Reflection

What I Learned

This was my first professional project — and the one that showed me the difference between building interfaces and understanding why they should be built a certain way. It planted the seed of design thinking that every project since has grown from.

🌀
Development fluency made me a better designer
Starting as a UI Developer gave me an instinct for what’s feasible and what creates technical debt. When I moved into UX, I designed with implementation in mind — which made handoff smoother and reduced back-and-forth with engineering.
📊
Data changes the conversation
Working with VoC survey data for the first time showed me how research reframes opinion into evidence. When we said “pricing needs to be earlier,” it landed differently because 35 survey respondents said it — not just us.
🔍
Testing reveals what you can’t predict
We built two PDP concepts expecting one to clearly win. 6:4 was closer than expected. The tooltip approach won because of fewer steps — something we hadn’t weighted as the primary differentiator. Testing humbled my assumptions.
🌟
E-commerce UX is about confidence, not features
Users didn’t want more information — they wanted the right information at the right moment. Surfacing price on the category card, not after configuration, wasn’t a feature change. It was a trust decision.
👥
Transitioning roles takes intention
Moving from developer to designer wasn’t automatic. I had to proactively ask to be involved in research and wireframing, learn new tools, and earn trust by showing I understood both sides. That intentionality shaped who I am professionally.
🌎
Enterprise UX demands humility about scope
FedEx Office is a complex, multi-team product. My contribution was real and meaningful — but it was one piece of a much larger system. Learning to do excellent work within a defined scope, rather than overreaching it, is a discipline I carry into every project.
